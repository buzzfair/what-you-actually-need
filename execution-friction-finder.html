Execution Friction Finder

Find out why the things you know to do are not getting done.

Implementation problems are not usually about motivation or discipline. They are about friction — the specific kind that makes starting hard, continuing harder, and finishing feel uncertain. If you have good ideas that are not becoming real things, the issue is almost always structural. Not personal. This assessment helps you locate the friction.

Friction Self-Assessment

Question 1

When you try to start a project, what usually stops you first?

Note whether it is decision, setup, tools, clarity, or something else.

Question 2

Do you have a clear "done" definition for your current main project?

Vague completion criteria are one of the most common execution blockers.

Question 3

How many active projects are you currently trying to move forward?

If it is more than two, that is usually the problem.

Question 4

When you get stuck, what do you do?

Research, defer, pivot — or push through? Understanding your pattern matters.

Question 5

Is the thing you are trying to build within your current skill set, or does it require skills or tools you do not yet have?

This is a scaffolding question, not a capability insult.

Question 6

Do you have a regular protected time block for implementation work?

Not "I work when I can." A specific, defended time block.

Question 7

Is there someone who can review, react, or hold you accountable?

Execution is faster with a witness. Isolation compounds friction.

Where execution gets stuck

  • Unclear scope — you do not know what done looks like
  • Tool confusion — you spend more time on setup than on output
  • Perfectionism as a gate — waiting until conditions are right
  • No protected time — execution happens in the margins and never accumulates
  • Missing scaffolding — you need something built that you do not know how to build
  • Decision debt — unresolved earlier choices that block later ones

What scaffolding is missing

Most implementation problems fall into one of three categories:

Technical scaffolding

You need a tool, system, or build that you do not have yet. This is the fastest to solve.

Process scaffolding

You need a sequence or structure to follow. Often a template, workflow, or checklist.

Accountability scaffolding

You need someone to be doing this with or for. Execution is a social act more often than people admit.

Identify which type of scaffolding is missing. That tells you whether you need to learn, hire, build, or delegate.
